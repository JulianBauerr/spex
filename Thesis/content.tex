\section{Basics}
\subsection{Quantum States}
In an $n$-dimensional Hilbert space with a chosen orthonormal basis, a quantum state, known as a ket vector $|\psi\rangle$, can be represented as an $n$-dimensional column vector $\vec{\psi}$:

\begin{equation}
	|\psi\rangle \doteq \vec{\psi} = \begin{pmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

Its corresponding dual state, known as a bra vector $\langle\psi|$, is represented by the conjugate transpose of this column vector, resulting in an $n$-dimensional row vector $\vec{\psi}^\dagger$:
\begin{equation}
	\langle\psi| \doteq \vec{\psi}^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} c_1^* & c_2^* & \dots & c_n^* \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
\cite[p.16f.]{Kottmann2025}
\cite[p.10f.]{sakurai2020modern} \\ \\
All quantum states are always normalized on the amplitude. This is to ensure the consistency with the probability interpretation. For a quantum state $\ket{\psi}$ applies
\begin{equation}
	\abs{c_0}^2 +\abs{c_1}^2 + \dots + \abs{c_n}^2 =1	
\end{equation}
\cite[p. 5]{Kottmann2025}




\subsection{Operators and Observables}
An operator acts on a ket from left and outputs another ket vector. Also an operator can act on a bra from the right and outputs another bra vector.
\begin{equation}
	X\ket{\alpha} = \ket{\beta}.
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	\bra{\alpha}X = \bra{\beta}.
\end{equation}

The operator X is called the \textbf{null operator}, if for any ket $\ket{\alpha}$ applies
\begin{equation}
	X \ket{\alpha} = 0.
\end{equation}

Operators can be added
\begin{equation}
	X + Y = Y + X.
\end{equation}

and also multiplied. In general they are noncommutative, that is,
\begin{equation}
	XY \neq YX.
\end{equation}
but they are assoziative
\begin{equation}
	X(YZ) = (XY )Z= XYZ.
\end{equation} \cite[p.11, 13-15]{sakurai2020modern} \\ \\
Because quantum states can be represented as vectors in $\mathbb{C}^2$. An operator can be represented by a complex matrix $U$. Given the input ket $\ket{\psi}$ the output of the operation $\ket{\phi}$ can be calculated as
\begin{equation}
	\ket{\phi} = U\ket{\psi}
\end{equation}
The state needs to be normalized, therefore we cannot allow all matrices, but only unitary matrices. This ensures that normalication. 
\cite[p. 19]{Kottmann2025}
